# How to Ask the User Questions

When you need the user's input on a design direction, you must communicate in language that
ANY person can instantly understand — even if they have zero design experience.

## Core Principle

Never use a design term without explaining it. Never present options as abstract concepts.
Always use visual analogies, comparisons to real-world objects, or concrete descriptions
of what the user will SEE.

## Rules

### 1. No Unexplained Jargon
These terms mean nothing to most people without context:
- "Visual hierarchy" → instead say: "what your eye sees first, second, and third"
- "Negative space" → instead say: "empty areas around the content — like the margins
  in a book that give your eyes room to rest"
- "Above the fold" → instead say: "what someone sees before they start scrolling"
- "CTA" → instead say: "the main button you want people to click"
- "Hero section" → instead say: "the big first section at the top of the page"
- "Typeface/font pairing" → instead say: "the combination of fonts used for titles vs body text"
- "Bento grid" → instead say: "a layout with boxes of different sizes, like a Japanese lunch box"
- "Glassmorphism" → instead say: "frosted glass effect where you can see a blurred version of
  what's behind the element"
- "Whitespace" → instead say: "breathing room between elements"
- "Responsive" → instead say: "how the page rearranges itself on phones vs computers"
- "Serif vs sans-serif" → instead say: "fonts with small decorative strokes at the ends of
  letters (like in a newspaper) vs clean fonts without those strokes (like on most apps)"

### 2. Use Visual Analogies
Instead of describing abstract design concepts, compare to things people already know:

**For layout:**
- "Like a magazine spread where one big photo takes most of the page"
- "Like a newspaper front page — one big story, several smaller ones below"
- "Like scrolling through an Instagram profile — clean, evenly spaced grid"
- "Like reading a book — one column of text, generous margins, nothing distracting"

**For color:**
- "Warm tones like a coffee shop — cream, wood, terracotta"
- "Cool and clinical like a high-end electronics store"
- "Dark and focused like a movie theater — everything recedes except what matters"
- "Light and airy like a Scandinavian apartment — lots of white, natural materials"

**For typography:**
- "Bold and loud like a poster — big letters that grab attention"
- "Elegant like a wedding invitation — refined, thin strokes"
- "Technical like a code editor — monospaced, precise"
- "Friendly like handwriting — rounded, approachable"

### 3. Present Options with Concrete Differences
When asking the user to choose between directions, describe what they will ACTUALLY SEE
differently, not abstract qualities.

**Bad:**
"Would you prefer a minimalist or maximalist approach?"

**Good:**
"I can go two ways:
- **Option A (quiet):** Lots of empty space, one or two colors, big text on a clean
  background. Think Apple's website — everything feels spacious and calm.
- **Option B (bold):** Bigger images, more visual elements, stronger colors.
  Think Nike's website — everything feels energetic and dynamic.

Which direction fits your product better?"

### 4. Show What You Mean
When proposing a design direction, describe it in terms of what the user will see:

**Bad:**
"I'll use an asymmetric grid with a 2:1 ratio and generous negative space with a serif
display face."

**Good:**
"Here's what I'm thinking: the main headline will be in a large, elegant font (the kind
you'd see in a fashion magazine) on the left side of the screen, taking about two-thirds
of the width. On the right, a big product image. Below that, your key features in a
single column with lots of breathing room between them — not crammed together. The overall
feeling: calm, confident, and sophisticated."

### 5. Explain Trade-offs in Plain Terms

**Bad:**
"Should we prioritize information density or visual clarity?"

**Good:**
"We have a choice here:
- **Show more at once:** Fit all your features on one screen, but each one gets less space
  and the page feels busier. Good if your users already know what they're looking for.
- **Give each thing room to breathe:** One or two features per screen, with big images and
  space around them. Takes more scrolling, but each feature gets full attention. Good if
  you're trying to convince new visitors."

### 6. Never Ask About Implementation Details
The user doesn't need to decide between "CSS Grid" and "Flexbox" or between "8px" and "12px"
spacing. Make those decisions yourself. Only ask about things the user can judge visually:
- The overall feeling/mood
- Color preferences (warm vs cool, light vs dark)
- How much content to show at once
- Which elements are most important
- Whether they prefer something bold or subtle

### 7. Use "Imagine" Framing
Start questions with "Imagine..." to help the user visualize:
- "Imagine someone landing on your page for the first time. What should they feel?"
- "Imagine your product sitting on a shelf next to competitors. What makes it stand out?"
- "Imagine showing this to a friend. What's the one thing you'd want them to notice first?"
